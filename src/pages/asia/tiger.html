Common name: Bengal Tiger

Latin name: Panthera tigris tigris

The Bengal tiger is a subspecies of tiger primarily found in India and Bangladesh.

About Bengal Tigers

The Bengal tiger is the most numerous tiger subspecies in Asia, with a population estimated at around 2,500. They are known for their distinctive orange coat with black stripes, which help them camouflage in their forest habitats.

Bengal tigers are carnivores and primarily prey on large mammals such as deer, wild pigs, and buffalo. They are solitary animals, marking their territories with urine and scent markings.

Conservation Status

The Bengal tiger is classified as Endangered by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN). Threats to their survival include habitat loss due to deforestation, poaching for their body parts, and conflicts with humans.

Conservation efforts include establishing protected areas such as national parks and reserves, anti-poaching measures, and community-based initiatives to reduce human-tiger conflicts.
